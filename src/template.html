The Anatomy of Ideas

A note before you wander

How to read this

This is one map, drawn from one vantage — not a neutral survey. It leans, by temperament, toward the traditions of detachment and withdrawal (Stoic, Buddhist, Daoist); the counter-case for engagement, relation and care is present but thinner. Read it as a perspective to argue with, not an authority to defer to.

The links between ideas — “builds on”, “rejects”, “echoes” — are interpretive. They are the author's readings of how thoughts relate, and reasonable scholars would draw some of them differently. The “argues against” foil on each entry is a useful provocation, not a claim that the two thinkers saw themselves as opponents.

These debates are live, not settled

Several ideas here are presented among genuine rivals. The “Contested” tag you'll see on some entries is scoped narrowly: it flags claims that arrive in the dress of established empirical science but lack consensus — the theories of consciousness (integrated-information, global-workspace, illusionist and idealist accounts) above all. Religious and metaphysical claims aren't tagged this way; their tradition badge already says whose claim it is. Idealism versus materialism and the freedom of the will are open questions too. Where this map states one, it is taking a side, not reporting a consensus.

Philosophy in stories

Some ideas were thought first in stories. Tragedy understood fate before the Stoics formalised it; the novel mapped the modern self before psychology did. Entries badged “Literature & Imagination” mark ideas whose native form is the artwork — enacted in a character, image or plot rather than argued in a treatise. They earn their place by the same rule as every other idea here: a single thesis sharp enough to name. The badge marks a method, not a lesser rank.

What it leaves out

The coverage is uneven, and some gaps remain:

  • Women are still under-represented, though the literary canon and figures like Simone de Beauvoir have begun to close the gap.
  • African and Indigenous philosophy now have a dedicated lane — Ubuntu, Àṣẹ, Amerindian perspectivism, the sacred land, Aboriginal law — added on a strict bar: each carries a named modern author and a real dated text, with no outsider ethnography standing in as authority. Korean thought joins the East lane on the same bar (the Four-Seven Debate, Wŏnhyo, Jinul, Donghak); the scripture-born Abrahamic core (Agape, Covenant, Imago Dei, Tawhid…) and other ancient civilizations (Zoroastrian Persia, Nahua Mesoamerica, Gilgamesh, Egyptian ma'at) now enter too. The Hermetic & Esoteric current (As Above So Below, Gnosis, alchemy, the Perennial Philosophy) is included as historically influential — mapped, not endorsed; like every religious and metaphysical claim here, it is disclosed by its tradition badge rather than singled out for a warning. Genuinely oral and shamanic cosmologies remain largely absent — and that absence is the map's own edge, not theirs. This atlas admits an idea only with a named author and a dated text; that bar keeps the honesty tight, but it is also a literacy filter. Traditions carried in speech, song, ritual and apprenticeship leave no dated treatise to cite, so the very rule that protects the rest of the map cannot see them — they are missing because of where this map draws its line, not because the thinking is thin.
  • It remains weighted toward Western and South-Asian sources.

The facts — datings, attributions, titles — have been checked across the whole atlas, but errors will remain. If you find one, treat it as the map's, not the territory's, and tell me.

Best explored on a desktop or tablet — the maps are built around a wide canvas, and a small phone screen will feel cramped.

Where to begin

If the whole map feels like too many doors at once, here is one suggested way in — ten texts, each opening a different question. It is the maintainer's pick, not a canon; argue with it, and click any one to meet the idea it carries.
